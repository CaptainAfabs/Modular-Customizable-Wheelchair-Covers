# Assembling a disc

This guide covers joining 8 printed segments into a single rigid disc, **before** you mount it on the wheel. Mounting is covered in [INSTALLATION.md](INSTALLATION.md).

**Time:** About 15 minutes per disc once you've done it once.

**You need:**
- 8 printed segments (per disc)
- ~16 zip ties (3 mm × 100 mm) — two per seam
- Flush cutters

---

## Step 1 — Lay out the segments

Lay the 8 segments **face down on a flat surface** in a rough circle, with the tongue edge of each segment pointing the same way (e.g., clockwise). Each segment has:

- An **arcuate outer edge** (the curved side that will form the rim of the disc).
- An **inner edge** (a short straight piece that, with the other 7 segments, forms the central aperture for the hub).
- A **tongue edge** on one radial side and a **groove edge** on the other.
- Multiple **apertures** (round holes) and **slots** along the edges for zip ties.

> **Why face down?** When you flip the assembled disc onto the wheel, the side that was facing you during assembly becomes the **inboard** side (the side facing the spokes). All zip-tie heads should end up on the inboard side so the outboard face stays clean.

## Step 2 — Engage the first seam

Pick two adjacent segments. Slide the **tongue** of one into the **groove** of the other so the radial edges meet flush. You should feel a positive click or slight friction; the tongue is intentionally slightly undersized to allow for printer tolerance.

If the tongue is too tight: sand it gently along its length with 200-grit sandpaper.
If it's too loose: you can still secure the seam with ties; the fit will tighten as you add more segments.

## Step 3 — Tie the seam

Each seam uses **two zip ties** — one closer to the centre, one closer to the rim.

1. Thread a zip tie through the matching aperture pair that **bridges the seam** (one hole in each segment). The tie should enter from the side facing you (which will be inboard) and exit on the same side.
2. Loop the tie back around the bridge between the two holes and feed the tail into the head.
3. Pull tight by hand. Don't over-tighten — you want the seam snug, not crushed.
4. Repeat for the second tie at a different radius.

> **Why two ties per seam?** A single tie can pivot around itself; two ties at different radii prevent the seam from rotating under hand impact.

## Step 4 — Repeat around the disc

Add segments one at a time. Each new segment engages the tongue of the previous one and provides a groove for the next. After segment 8 you'll close the loop by tying segment 8 back to segment 1.

> **Tip:** When closing the last seam, you may need to flex two segments apart slightly to get the tongue into the groove. The seam fasteners on either side will hold the deflection elastically; this is normal.

## Step 5 — Check the disc

Lift the assembled disc by the rim. It should:

- Feel rigid in the plane of the disc (no flex side-to-side).
- Have an even circular outer edge with no visible step at any seam.
- Have a clean central aperture roughly centred on the disc.
- Have **all 16 zip-tie heads on the same side** (the side that will become inboard).

If a seam shows a step or gap, loosen its two ties, re-seat the tongue, and re-tighten.

## Step 6 — Trim the tails

Use **flush cutters** to snip every zip-tie tail as close to the head as possible. Run a finger over each one — there should be **no sharp edge**. If you feel a burr, trim again.

> **Why flush trim matters:** The tails sit on the inboard side, close to the user's lap and clothing. A snagged tail can tear pants or scratch skin. Flush cutters and a careful touch are the difference between a comfortable cover and a hazardous one.

## You're done

You now have one assembled disc. Repeat for the second wheel before moving on to [INSTALLATION.md](INSTALLATION.md).
